Abstract: We show that a diffusion sampler for lattice-field configurations can be trained without preparing training data by an external Monte Carlo calculation. Starting from exactly sampled configurations at β = 0 , we construct a self-bootstrap sampler, SLDiffusion, in which periodic Gaussian proposals with a fixed learned score are Metropolis–Hastings corrected, at each β, against the same physical target at every noise level, and only replay configurations from the resulting chain are used to train the score at the next stage. In the two-dimensional compact XY model, self-training proceeds from β = 0 .30 to 0.50 at L = 4 . At β = 0 .5, the energy and vortex densities for L = 4 , 6, 8, 12 agree with independent Hybrid Monte Carlo calculations within 1.35 σ. Volume-native retraining at L = 8 and 12 improves both the proposal displacement and autocorrelation. The integrated autocorrelation times of the energy and vortex densities remain below two for all volumes studied. These results demonstrate that a Metropolis-corrected diffusion sampler can be self-trained without configurations drawn in advance from the target coupling.

## 1 Introduction 

In strongly coupled quantum field theories, vacuum structure, bound states, and finite-temperature phase transitions cannot in general be determined from perturba-tion theory alone. Confinement, hadron masses, and the phase structure of quantum chromodynamics (QCD) at finite temperature and density are prominent examples. Lattice field theory regularizes spacetime on a lattice and defines the Euclidean path integral as a finite-dimensional statistical integral [1, 2]. Many nonperturbative cal-culations therefore reduce to generating field configurations ϕ from the Boltzmann distribution 

π(ϕ) = 1

Z exp[ −S(ϕ)] (1.1) specified by an action S, and estimating observables by Monte Carlo averages. Hybrid Monte Carlo (HMC), which combines global molecular-dynamics propos-als with a Metropolis correction, is a standard lattice algorithm [3]. As the lattice spacing is reduced or the volume is increased, slow modes of the Markov chain can persist and increase the integrated autocorrelation time. Near a critical point this behavior is commonly written as τint ∝ ξz , where the dynamic critical exponent z

depends on the theory, update algorithm, and observable [4–6]. Freezing of topolog-ical degrees of freedom is another important issue toward the continuum limit [7, 8]. These difficulties motivate new proposal mechanisms that move correlated degrees of freedom while preserving the known target distribution. Self-learning Monte Carlo (SLMC) was introduced as a framework in which an effective model learned from preliminary simulations generates global candidates, while an acceptance test using the original model preserves exactness [9, 10]. In self-learning Hybrid Monte Carlo (SLHMC), molecular-dynamics trajectories are generated using a neural potential, corrected by a Metropolis test with the origi-nal first-principles energy, and the potential is updated from accepted configurations [11]. This idea has been extended to SLMC for non-Abelian lattice gauge theory with dynamical fermions, SLHMC with gauge-covariant neural networks, and SLMC with equivariant Transformers [12–14]. In these self-learning architectures, a neural surrogate supplies efficient proposals and an exact MCMC acceptance step preserves – 1 – the target distribution. The accepted chain can thus produce its own subsequent training data without requiring a separate, precomputed data set. The present work transfers this principle to a score-based diffusion proposal. Neural samplers based on normalizing flows, diffusion models, and equivariant neural networks have recently been introduced in lattice field theory [15–20]. Equiv-ariant neural architectures for lattice systems and applications to lattice gauge fixing have also been developed [21, 22]. Denoising diffusion probabilistic models and score-based generative models, originally developed for image generation [23–25], primarily approximate an unknown data distribution. In lattice field theory, by contrast, the unnormalized weight exp[ −S(ϕ)] can be evaluated for every configuration even when the partition function Z is unknown. A learned generator can therefore be supple-mented by a Metropolis–Hastings (MH) correction based on the known action and proposal density, rather than being used directly for measurements. Lattice samplers based on normalizing flows evaluate a model density from an invertible map and its Jacobian, and use it in independence MH or reweighting [15– 17, 26–34]. Related approaches approximate a trivializing map by a flow [35–39], or use forward-to-reverse path ratios in a stochastic normalizing flow [40, 41]. The noise-conditioned MAALA of Zhu et al. starts from a standard-normal prior, uses the high-noise region as unadjusted annealed Langevin transport, and applies MH correction only to the final low-noise steps [42]. In contrast, SLDiffusion-MAALA starts from the current physical configuration and applies an MH test against the same physical target at every noise level. We refer to this construction as the fully Metropolized MAALA variant. Its noise ladder changes the proposal drift and vari-ance while keeping the invariant distribution fixed, rather than transporting configu-rations between intermediate noised distributions. This distinction does not by itself establish which construction mixes faster, nor does it establish a general alleviation of critical slowing down. Diffusion and MAALA samplers for lattice field theory have combined a noise-conditioned score, annealed Langevin proposals, forward and reverse proposal prob-abilities, and MH correction [18, 19, 42–45]. The score is the gradient of a log prob-ability density and plays a role analogous to the HMC force or Langevin drift. For finite-step MALA or MAALA, preservation of the target distribution is ensured by the MH ratio, including the reverse proposal probability, rather than by the accuracy of the score approximation [46]. Previous lattice diffusion samplers have trained the score on reference config-urations produced by an external Monte Carlo calculation at the target coupling [18, 19, 42–44]. The near-critical and cross-volume study of Ref. [19], for example, uses reference ensembles from HMC, Fourier-accelerated HMC, and Wolff updates [47]. This strategy requires a sufficiently large, equilibrated sample from the target distribution before the neural sampler can be trained. Repeating that preparation for every coupling and volume leaves a substantial part of the configuration-generation – 2 – task in the training-data pipeline. Moreover, if the reference chain does not ade-quately cover a slow mode, the learned proposal can inherit that deficiency. Starting without external target-distribution samples and updating the training data from the generated chain itself removes this circular requirement. To this end, we propose the self-learning diffusion sampler SLDiffusion. Our aim here is not to demonstrate a general solution to critical slowing down, but to establish a bootstrap mechanism for applying diffusion models, which are promising tools for mitigating it, to lattice configuration generation without external training configurations. We start from the exactly sampleable distribution at β = 0 . At each nearby value of β, a MAALA chain is generated with a fixed neural score, and only unweighted replay configurations from the MH-corrected chain are used to train the score at the next stage. The provenance of every training configuration can therefore be traced back to β = 0 . The novelty lies not in MAALA or the MH correction themselves, but in this self-bootstrap beta ladder, which removes the need for external training configurations at the target coupling. As a proof of concept, we study the high-temperature phase of the two-dimensional compact XY model. In our action convention, the Berezinskii– Kosterlitz–Thouless (BKT) transition occurs at βBKT ≃ 1.12 [48]. We advance the beta ladder from β = 0 .30 to 0.50 at L = 4 , and at β = 0 .5 study L = 6 , 8, 12 .We test whether training can continue using only self-generated replay, whether observables measured after MH correction agree with an independent calculation, and whether volume-native retraining controls autocorrelation. For L = 4 , 6, 8, 12 ,the integrated autocorrelation times of the energy and vortex densities, measured in units of saved trajectories, are shorter than those of HMC; at L = 12 , the corresponding autocorrelation functions also decay faster in the same units. One SLDiffusion-MAALA trajectory, however, contains 32 full-lattice updates and score evaluations, whereas the HMC comparison uses ϵ = 0 .5, two leapfrog steps, and trajectory length one at every volume without volume-specific retuning. This is therefore a comparison of correlations along saved trajectories, not of computational efficiency. The representational capacity and run time of diffusion models near criticality are separate from the self-training mechanism addressed here. Section 2 defines the model and observables. Section 3 describes the self-bootstrap beta ladder and the SLDiffusion-MAALA transition kernel. Sections 4 and 5 present the numerical setup and results, and Sec. 6 discusses volume depen-dence and the scope of the method. 3.1 Exactly sampleable β = 0 distribution

3.4 Replay selection 

A replay chain must satisfy at least the following conditions before it is used for the next training stage: sufficient statistics; finite and suitably small τint for the energy and vortex densities; consistency of the Schwinger–Dyson temperature with the tar-get β; no significant drift in the energy or vortex density after the post-thermalization series is divided into three consecutive intervals; and traceable provenance of the saved replay back to the preceding stage. The three-way split tests for residual non-stationary evolution within the measurement window after thermalization. Passing it means that no evidence for insufficient thermalization is observed for the tested quantities at the available precision; it does not by itself prove complete thermaliza-tion or ergodicity. The diagnostics are defined in App. B.5. Acceptance is not used to decide whether the replay distribution is correct. A low acceptance rate reduces efficiency but does not change the invariant distribution of an MH-corrected chain. Acceptance and MSJD thresholds are, however, imposed before advancing to the next coupling, as specified in App. B.5. For the fixed network used – 9 – in measurement, we also examine acceptance, MSJD, autocorrelation, temperature, stationarity, and the score error on validation data. Comparisons with HMC are performed only after these conditions have been fixed. 

3.5 Post-training configuration generation 

In the fully Metropolized variant used here, after training and replay selection have been completed at the target coupling β = βK , we fix the score-network parameters, the β input, the noise ladder, the step scale, and the image sum for the wrapped Gaussian. Let θ(n) denote the current Markov state, and start from θ0 = θ(n). At each noise level i = 1 , . . . , M , a candidate eθi is generated using Eq. (3.10). If it is accepted according to Eq. (3.12), we set θi = eθi; otherwise, we retain the preceding state, θi = θi−1. After one complete pass through the noise ladder, the next Markov state is 

θ(n+1) = θM . (3.13) These M updates constitute one trajectory. Repeating this trajectory generates the SLDiffusion-MAALA measurement chain. For the measurement chain, a prespecified thermalization interval is discarded, after which the states θ(n) are saved at the prescribed interval. In this paper, “gen-erated configurations” are the configurations saved from this post-thermalization, MH-corrected chain. During measurement, we do not retrain the network, mod-ify the transition kernel by adding replay, use HMC for any correction, or perform reweighting. The trained network therefore does not produce independent configu-rations from noise. Instead, a fixed MH-corrected transition kernel containing the neural drift generates a Markov chain whose invariant distribution is the target dis-tribution.

Methods based on external samples from the target distribution must generate a sufficient training ensemble before learning begins. Where a conventional sampler is slow, this burden remains in the construction of the training data. SLDiffusion reverses that order by alternating MH-corrected sampling and score training from an exact initial distribution. Thermalization, stationarity, and coverage of modes must nevertheless be examined at every stage. 

6.2 Learned drift and the equilibrium distribution 

A SLDiffusion-MAALA trajectory consists of one learned-score proposal and one MH test at each noise level. Each test uses the action difference and the forward and reverse proposal probabilities. The drift changes the direction and distance of chain movement, and its effect appears in acceptance, MSJD, thermalization time, and integrated autocorrelation time. The MH test keeps the equilibrium distribution of the fixed kernel equal to the target Boltzmann distribution. The test at each noise level is the standard MALA correction [46]. Whereas the MAALA of Zhu et al. corrects the final low-noise steps, the variant used here corrects every level [42, 44]. This difference changes the transport mechanism, while invariance of the fixed production kernel follows from the level-wise MH ratios. The unadjusted high-noise transport of Zhu et al. provides a mechanism for large moves, whereas our high-noise proposals are also subject to the MH test against the physical action. Their relative mixing depends on the theory, volume, score, and noise schedule. This construction difference alone establishes neither a general ordering of their mixing nor a general improvement of critical slowing down. A detailed comparison is given – 19 – in App. B.3. A finite chain must therefore be assessed not only through detailed balance but also through thermalization and transitions between modes. For finite chains, we combine the Schwinger–Dyson temperature, the three-way split of the measurement series, histograms, and autocorrelation functions. These nu-merical diagnostics do not replace the invariance argument; they test thermalization and mixing for specific observables.

The learned score changes Pϕ through the proposal probability, and can therefore modify the mixing encoded in λn and cn(O) without changing the equilibrium distribution after MH correction. Autocorrelation can increase when the target distribution differs substantially from the replay distribution. This occurs, for example, when the beta increment is too large, a small-volume network is transferred directly to a larger volume, or replay underrepresents a mode. The resulting mismatch between the learned drift and the target score can increase rejected self-transitions or fail to propose transitions along a slow mode. Conversely, steps that are too small can produce high acceptance but low MSJD. Acceptance, MSJD, and the observable-dependent τint must therefore be considered together, especially for infrared modes, vortices, and winding sectors [55]. Retraining the score reduces the mismatch in the proposal drift. With generation parameters fixed, volume-native retraining at L = 8 and 12 increases acceptance and MSJD and decreases the estimated autocorrelation times of both observables. Thus autocorrelation is not an intrinsic limitation of the diffusion form; it can be changed by a neural drift trained on MH-corrected replay. A network alone need not remove every slow mode, however. Its receptive field, the modes represented in replay, and the resolution of both the beta and noise ladders also matter. 6.5 Increasing volume and volume-native retraining 

At L = 8 and 12 , β = 0 .5, transferring the learned drift from a smaller volume with-out retraining lowers acceptance and MSJD and increases autocorrelation times. Retraining on MH-corrected replay at the target volume improves all three diagnos-tics. Continuing self-training from the target-volume chain, rather than ending with zero-shot transfer, is therefore effective in restoring proposal performance. For proposals that move the full lattice at once, local score errors and action differences can accumulate with volume. The asymptotic analysis of MALA for in-dependent components gives a known V −1/3 scaling of the step-size-like proposal variance [62]. This exponent cannot be transferred directly to a correlated lattice system with multilevel MAALA, but it provides a guide for jointly adjusting the per-level coefficient a = A/M , the number of levels M , and the noise range as the volume grows. The overlap between adjacent Boltzmann distributions is also controlled ap-proximately by (∆ β)2 Var( C). Where Var( C) ∝ V , maintaining comparable overlap suggests ∆β ∝ V −1/2. The initial β = 0 → 0.30 interval used at L = 4 should therefore not automatically be retained at larger volume; the beta ladder should be subdivided according to acceptance, MSJD, βSD , and effective sample size. The same score-network architecture is used at every volume. Our results concern the self-training mechanism and the measured observables, not a ranking of the representational capacity of diffusion models. An application near the BKT point would require separate tests of infrared quantities, including the helicity modulus, winding sectors, the zero mode, and long-wavelength Fourier modes. 

6.6 Relation to flow-based methods 

An independence proposal based on a normalizing flow evaluates a normalized model density and Jacobian for use in an independent Metropolis test or direct estima-tor [15, 16]. In contrast, SLDiffusion-MAALA is a state-dependent local Gaussian – 21 – transition and uses the forward and reverse transition probabilities at every step. Stochastic normalizing flows correct endpoints using nonequilibrium path weights or work [40, 41]; here, each MAALA transition is MH corrected and observables are measured from the resulting unweighted Markov chain. SLDiffusion thus uses a fixed MH-corrected Markov kernel rather than a global generative density. 

## 7 Conclusion 

We have proposed SLDiffusion, a method for self-training a Metropolis-corrected diffusion sampler without external training configurations from the target coupling. The procedure begins at the exactly sampleable β = 0 distribution, generates a chain with a fixed SLDiffusion-MAALA kernel at each coupling, and trains the next score using only replay from the MH-corrected chain. During physics measurements, the network and generation parameters are fixed, and the Markov chain includes an MH correction with both forward and reverse wrapped-Gaussian proposal densities. For the two-dimensional compact XY model, self-training continues from β =0.30 to 0.50 at L = 4 . At L = 4 , 6, 8, 12 and β = 0 .5, the energy and vortex densities from thermalized measurement chains agree within statistical uncertainties with independent HMC calculations. Proposal performance decreases when trans-ferring to L = 8 and 12 , but volume-native retraining on the MH-corrected replay increases acceptance and MSJD and reduces the integrated autocorrelation times. The Schwinger–Dyson temperature, split-series tests, histograms, and autocorrela-tion functions reveal no anomaly in the self-trained chains over the range studied. In the analytic-action-force controls, the 32-level trajectory has acceptance and auto-correlation comparable to or better than the learned-score trajectory, while one-step MALA is more efficient per full-lattice update. The contribution here is therefore not a performance advantage of the learned score, but its construction without external target training configurations. Denoising score matching, noise-conditioned Langevin proposals, and the MH correction of MALA or MAALA are established methods [25, 42, 44, 46, 59]. The measurement kernel used here is a fully Metropolized MAALA variant based on the noise-conditioned construction of Zhu et al. It differs from their generation algorithm, which leaves high-noise transport unadjusted and corrects the final low-noise steps. The distinguishing feature of SLDiffusion is that its score is trained on self-generated replay descending from an exact initial distribution, removing the preliminary step of preparing teacher configurations at the target coupling. Applications to larger volumes and the vicinity of the BKT transition will require volume-adapted beta and noise ladders together with direct tests of infrared observables and winding-sector mixing. – 22 – Data and code availability

B.2 Training and generation 

The loss is denoising score matching on angular fields perturbed by wrapped Gaussian noise [25, 59]. At each stage, the MH-corrected replay promoted from the preceding stage is divided into training and validation sets, and the network is trained for 100 full passes over replay. We use Adam [66], with a default learning rate of 10 −3. The mini-batch size is chosen according to replay size and volume. Parameters at the checkpoint with minimum validation loss are used for the measurement kernel. Training noise is selected from the eight values in Eq. (3.6). Generation uses a 32-level noise-scale ladder geometrically interpolated from 0.80 to 0.05 , with one wrapped-Gaussian MALA proposal at each level and an MH test against the same physical target. Both forward and reverse proposal densities are evaluated in the MH test of Eq. (3.12). The wrapped-Gaussian infinite sum is evaluated after mapping angular differences to the principal branch and summing images n = −3, . . . , 3. The same image range is used for the training score and both generation densities, and the omitted probability is below 10 −14 at the largest variance used. The beta ladder is the sequence of couplings along which self-training proceeds; the noise ladder changes the proposal drift and variance within one trajectory. It is not unadjusted transport between intermediate noised distributions.

B.3 Comparison with the original noise-conditioned MAALA 

The SLDiffusion-MAALA kernel used here builds on the noise-conditioned score and noise ladder of the DM–MAALA of Zhu et al. [42], but it is not the same Markov-kernel construction. ## C Invariance and ergodicity of the fixed transition kernel 

C.1 Invariance of the fixed transition kernel 

Invariance and convergence conditions for the Metropolis-adjusted Langevin algo-rithm (MALA) are standard results in Markov chain Monte Carlo theory [4, 46]. Noise-conditioned Langevin proposals with Metropolis correction have also been used – 29 – in lattice diffusion samplers [42, 44]. Zhu et al. leave the high-noise transport un-adjusted and correct the final low-noise steps. Here we impose the detailed balance relation below at every noise level, with the same πβ as the invariant distribution of each level. For physics measurements, the trained network, noise ladder, step scale, and wrapped-density evaluation are fixed. The transition at level i then satisfies 

πβ (θ)qi(θ′ | θ)P (i)acc (θ′ | θ) = πβ (θ′)qi(θ | θ′)P (i)acc (θ | θ′). (C.1) The level-wise MH kernels and their composition therefore preserve πβ .This invariance does not assume that the learned score equals the true score. Score errors change acceptance, MSJD, thermalization time, and autocorrelation time, but not the invariant distribution as long as the correct forward and reverse densities are used in the MH ratio. Invariance alone does not establish that a finite thermalization reaches equilibrium or that the resulting samples are independent. 

C.2 Ergodicity 

The configuration space of the two-dimensional XY model is an L2-dimensional torus. The wrapped Gaussian noise in Eq. (3.10) has nonzero variance in every degree of freedom and gives a positive proposal density on the finite-volume torus. Standard MH theory therefore implies πβ -irreducibility, while rejection supplies self-transitions that make the kernel aperiodic [4, 46]. Reachability does not imply sufficiently rapid mixing at finite time. Unit tests check consistency of the forward and reverse proposal probabilities. We further combine the Schwinger–Dyson temperature, the three-way split of the measurement series, histograms, and autocorrelation functions, and compare representative ob-servables with independent HMC. No anomaly is observed in these diagnostics over the measured range, and the integrated autocorrelation times of the energy and vor-tex densities are below two at L = 4 , 6, 8, 12 , β = 0 .5. These are finite-statistics diagnostics for the measured observables, not a statement of uniform convergence of every mode or in the critical region.
