Abstract 

Score-based generative models and Langevin samplers rely on estimating the score function 

‚ąáx log pt(x) of a forward diffusion. Classically this is tractable when the drift is linear: the marginal density is Gaussian and the score is a global conditional expectation [3, 14]. For a general nonlinear, state-dependent drift the marginal density has no closed form, and existing methods‚ÄĒdenoising score matching (DSM) and global Fokker‚ÄďPlanck residual penalties [5]‚ÄĒ resort to global averaging that inflates estimation error in low-density regions precisely where accuracy is most critical. We address this by developing a local Fokker‚ÄďPlanck geometric framework that replaces global conditioning with local parabolic averaging. Our approach rests on three numerical-analytic contributions. First, a time change to the cumulative-variance coordinate reduces the variable-coefficient Fokker‚ÄďPlanck equation to a standard inhomogeneous heat equation, on which we extend Evans‚Äô classical heat-ball monotonicity method to derive exact local mean-value representations for the score ‚ąáx log p together with the density, log-density, and entropy density (Theorem 5, Corollary 6); local well-posedness is established under an explicit dimension-dependent drift budget (Theorem 1). Second, for high-dimensional Monte Carlo evaluation of the resulting heat-ball integrals, we introduce the őļ-measure‚ÄĒthe Watson mean-value proba-bility measure on heat balls‚ÄĒand derive its exact factorized sampler (Algorithm 1) with unit per-sample weight, Ōá22 radial concentration, and a provable ő©( ‚ąöd) variance advantage over uniform importance sampling (Corollary 13, Proposition 14). Third, the r ‚Üí 0 limit of the heat-ball residual recovers the pointwise Fokker‚ÄďPlanck residual (Theorem 8), showing that the local framework is a one-parameter generalization of global FP-residual methods, and that the DSM population minimizer is feasible for the heat-ball constraint at every scale. We validate the framework on 2D structured data‚ÄĒwhere the local constraint demonstrably outperforms the global FP residual in low-density regions‚ÄĒon 256-dimensional MNIST, and on a dedicated sampler study confirming the concentration laws of Corollary 13.

# 5 Numerical Validation 

We validate the local heat-ball (HB) framework along three complementary axes: (i) a controlled 2D study with known ground-truth score, where the theoretical predictions of Theorems 1‚Äď8 can be verified directly; (ii) a 256-dimensional test on binarized MNIST confirming that the őļ-measure sampler scales to moderate dimension; and (iii) the dedicated sampler study of Section 4.4 (Fig-ure 1), which validates the concentration laws of Corollary 13. The goal is to isolate the quantitative predictions of the theory; large-scale generative benchmarks are outside the scope of this work.

We have developed a local Fokker‚ÄďPlanck geometric framework for score estimation that replaces the global conditional expectation of denoising score matching with a local parabolic average. The central methodological advance is the extension of the classical heat-ball mean-value method (Watson [7], Evans [6]) to variable-coefficient Fokker‚ÄďPlanck dynamics via a cumulative-variance time change. This yields three categories of result. 

Analytical foundations. Theorem 5 and Corollary 6 provide exact local mean-value representa-tions for the density u, log-density v = log u, score ‚ąáv, and entropy density w = u log u as heat-ball integrals. Theorem 1 establishes local well-posedness with an explicit dimension-dependent drift budget‚ÄĒthe safe existence window shrinks like d‚ąí1, making drift control structurally necessary in high dimensions. Theorem 8 shows that the heat-ball framework is a strict generalization of both denoising score matching (whose population minimizer is feasible for the heat-ball constraint at all scales) and global FP-residual methods (whose pointwise residual is recovered as r ‚Üí 0). 

Computational contributions. The őļ-measure‚ÄĒthe Watson mean-value probability measure‚ÄĒ admits a closed-form factorization into independent Beta( d/ 2 + 1 , 1) radial, ŌÉ‚čÜ-concentrated tem-poral, and uniform spherical components (Proposition 11). Algorithm 1 samples it exactly with unit per-sample weight and no rejection. Corollary 13 proves Ōá22 radial and Gaussian temporal concentration; Proposition 14 proves a non-asymptotic ő©( ‚ąöd) variance advantage over uniform importance sampling. 

Numerical evidence. The 2D diagnostic study confirms two qualitative effects predicted by the theory: the local heat-ball constraint preserves low-density accuracy that the global FP residual degrades (Observation 1), and the Helmholtz decomposition provides an interpretable diagnostic of estimation artifacts in the learned drift (Observation 2). The 256-dimensional MNIST experiment confirms that the őļ-measure sampler scales to moderate dimension, and the dedicated sampler study validates the Ōá22 and Gaussian concentration laws of Corollary 13. All reported MSE/MMD values are single-seed and indicative; the primary contribution is the analytical and algorithmic framework, whose correctness and convergence properties are established by Theorems 1‚Äď8.
