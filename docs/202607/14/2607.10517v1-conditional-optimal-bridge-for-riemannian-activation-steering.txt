# Conditional Optimal Bridge for Riemannian Activation Steering 

Seyed Arshan Dalili Ajay Narayanan Sridhar Vijaykrishnan Narayanan Mehrdad Mahdavi The Pennsylvania State University {sbd5760,afs6372,vxn9,mzm616}@psu.edu 

Abstract 

Activation steering offers a lightweight alternative to fine-tuning for controlling large language models at inference time. While many existing methods implicitly optimize a log-density-ratio objective between desired and undesired activation distributions, they do so heuristically rather than deriving it from a principled optimization problem. Moreover, these methods produce query-independent steering directions that can degrade performance on both in-distribution and out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs. We introduce Cobras (Conditional Optimal Bridge for Riemannian Activation Steering), which addresses both limitations by casting activation steering as a Schrödinger Bridge on the residual-stream hypersphere. This formulation yields, to our knowledge, the first principled derivation of the log-density-ratio steering objective from a well-posed optimization problem. Solving the bridge via entropic optimal transport and extracting the probability flow ODE recovers the widely used density-ratio gradient as a special case when the Sinkhorn potentials are uniform. Crucially, the Schrödinger potentials are evaluated at the current activation, making the resulting steering direction inherently query-adaptive. Empirically, across four models and three alignment axes (helpfulness, truthfulness, and detoxification), Cobras consistently outperforms prior activation steering baselines while avoiding the OOD degradation commonly observed in existing methods. # 1 Introduction 

Activation steering, also termed representation engineering, has emerged as a compelling alternative to fine-tuning for shaping the behavior of large language models (LLMs) at inference time [Rimsky et al., 2024, Li et al., 2023, Wang et al., 2025]. Its empirical success is often motivated by the linear representation hypothesis [Park et al., 2023, Alain and Bengio, 2016] where many high-level properties encoded by an LLM, such as sentiment, truthfulness, or harmfulness, appear to correspond to approximately linear directions in activation space. Under this view, changing a model’s behavior need not require modifying its weights; it can instead be achieved by moving its internal activations along directions associated with the target property at inference time. A standard approach is activation addition, which modifies the original activations by adding either a fixed steering vector or one that depends on the current activation. This perspective has inspired a growing body of work on inference-time control through activation interventions. Early methods typically construct a single steering direction from contrastive examples and then apply that direction uniformly across queries [Rimsky et al., 2024], while more recent approaches refine the dynamics of the intervention by integrating a continuous steering field over multiple steps [Zhao et al., 2026, Wang et al., 2025, Rodriguez et al., 2025] or working on a more faithful manifold by rotating activations on the hypersphere You et al. [2026], Pham and Nguyen [2024]. These advances show that steering can be both effective and computationally lightweight. Despite this progress, existing methods share two limitations. First, most of the methods and their implicit objective are heuristic rather than derived from a well-posed optimization problem without formal justification for the objective itself. Second, these steering signals are query-independent . A steering vector that helps in-distribution prompts can make out-of-distribution (OOD) inputs wrong or gibberish, and our experiments confirm that several strong baselines improve TruthfulQA Lin et al. [2021] at the cost of severe MMLU Hendrycks et al. [2020] and GSM8K Cobbe et al. [2021] degradation. We address both 1arXiv:2607.10517v1 [cs.LG] 12 Jul 2026 gaps with Cobras , which casts steering as a Schrödinger Bridge, yielding both a principled derivation of the steering objective and an inherently query-adaptive steering direction. Overall, our contributions are: (i) we show that the Schrödinger Bridge on the activation hypersphere provides, to our knowledge, the first principled derivation of the implicitly widely used log-density-ratio steering objective from an optimization problem; (ii) we propose Cobras (Conditional Optimal Bridge for Riemannian Activation Steering), a practical algorithm that combines entropic optimal-transport potentials, spherical kernel density estimation, and a gating mechanism to produce query-adaptive steering directions; and (iii) we demonstrate that Cobras consistently outperforms prior baselines on helpfulness, truthfulness, and detoxification while avoiding the out-of-distribution degradation observed in existing methods. 

Organization. Section 2 discusses the related work. Section 3 elaborates on the background, notation, setup, and the assumption of the manifold where LLM activations live. Section 4 then formulates the steering problem and its objective as the Schrödinger Bridge instance, and then solves this problem and derives the objective for steering, and proposes Cobras . Section 5 then does extensive experiments for both in-distribution and OOD and shows that Cobras outperforms baselines in both settings. Finally, Section 6 concludes the paper, discussing limitations and future work. The proof of technical results and additional experiments are reported in the appendix. 

# 2 Related Work 

Theory of activation steering. Several recent works have sought to develop a unified theory of activation steering by interpreting interventions as structured transformations in representation space. Linear Activation Transportation [Rodriguez et al., 2025] formulates steering as a transport map between source and target activation distributions, LiSeCo [Cheng and Alonso, 2024] analyzes steering through linear separability and constrained optimization, and ODESteer [Zhao et al., 2026] views steering as a continuous-time dynamical process rather than a one-shot activation edit. These perspectives provide useful formal tools for reasoning about activation interventions. However, their guarantees are typically derived for surrogate objectives and under specific geometric constraints rather than from a principled perspective. Also, the Schrödinger Bridge (SB) problem has been widely adopted for generative modeling via forward–backward SDE solvers [De Bortoli et al., 2021, Chen et al., 2021, Shi et al., 2023]. Cobras is the first to apply this framework for LLM activation steering. 

Activation steering approaches. Early and widely used steering approaches Zou et al. [2023], Li et al. [2023], Rimsky et al. [2024] identify behavior-relevant directions in activation space and apply additive edits. More recent methods refine this basic recipe by accounting for activation geometry or distributional structure, for example through minimally invasive affine maps [Singh et al., 2024], norm-preserving rotations [Pham and Nguyen, 2024], transport-based maps [Rodriguez et al., 2025], hyperspherical interventions [You et al., 2026], or continuous dynamical updates [Zhao et al., 2026]. Despite these advances, most existing methods are query-independent: once a direction or transformation is learned, essentially the same intervention is applied across prompts. Prior work shows that such fixed interventions can have limited OOD generalization, induce off-target side effects, and degrade performance when steering is unnecessary [Tan et al., 2024, Raedler et al., 2025, Da Silva et al., 2025]. Our work instead proposes a query-adaptive steering method that modulates the intervention based on the input, preserving OOD performance while improving in-distribution performance.

# 4 LLM Steering as Schrödinger Bridge 

A growing body of steering methods Rimsky et al. [2024], Zhao et al. [2026], Li et al. [2023] implicitly construct steering directions that approximate the gradient of the log-density ratio between desired and undesired activations: 

v(x) ∝ ∇ log p+(x)

Nevertheless, Eq. 2 is typically adopted as a plausible objective rather than derived from an optimization problem. To our knowledge, no existing work formulates an optimization problem on the activation manifold whose solution provably yields the log-density-ratio gradient of Eq. 2. This gap raises a natural question: can such a optimization-based formulation be rigorously established? We show that the Schrödinger Bridge (SB) provides a generalized, principled framework for steering. Specifically, we cast steering as an SB problem between the two marginals p− and p+ and seek the optimal stochastic process that steers the distribution of hidden state from p− to p+ with minimal “effort,” measured as the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence from a reference stochastic process. The SB solution induces a stochastic differential equation (SDE). To find the drift of the corresponding SDE, we reduce SB to an entropic optimal transport and solve it using the iterative Sinkhorn algorithm by alternating between updating the dual potentials (Sec. 4.1). We then apply the probability flow ODE to the induced SDE drift to obtain a deterministic process with the same time marginals. The resulting ODE has drift proportional to ∇S log ( ˆψ/ ˆϕ), the Riemannian gradient of the log-ratio of two Schrödinger potentials (Sec. 4.2). Finally, we evaluate this drift at arbitrary query points using nonparametric kernel density estimation (Sec. 4.3), yielding the Cobras method (Sec. 4.4). This derivation has two key consequences. First, the heuristic Eq. 2 emerges as a well-defined limiting case of the SB log-ratio log ( ˆψ/ ˆϕ): when the Sinkhorn potentials are approximately uniform, ˆψ and ˆϕ

reduce to kernel density estimates of p+ and p−, and the drift becomes ∇S log (p+/p −) (Prop.1). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first principled derivation of the log-density-ratio steering objective from a rigorous optimization problem. Second, because the Schrödinger potentials are evaluated at the current activation, the resulting steering is inherently context dependent. Different queries receive different steering directions, with the weights adapting to the query’s location and naturally attenuating intervention for inputs far from the training, thus supporting OOD queries.

4.4 The Overall Algorithm 

Given the vector field in Eq. 19, we steer a query activation hq by integrating the probability flow ODE at Eq. 12 on S using K geodesic Euler steps. At each step, we evaluate the field at the current iterate and update via the exponential map in Eq. 1, ensuring that every iterate remains on S. After integration, we restore the original norm ∥hq ∥. Algorithm 1 summarizes the full procedure. The algorithm operates in two distinct phases: an offline optimization stage (Lines 1-3) to solve the static SB problem and an online inference stage (Lines 4-10) that evolves a query activation along a probability flow. In the offline phase, it projects sample data onto a hypersphere and solves an entropic optimal transport problem via the Sinkhorn algorithm to derive Schrödinger potentials. During the online phase, the algorithm iteratively steers a query activation hq by integrating a probability flow ODE. It calculates a velocity vector vk in the tangent space based on weighted geodesic distances between positive and negative samples, updating the intermediate state qk via the exponential map. # 5 Experiments 

We conduct experiments to answer the following research questions. (RQ1): How does Cobras 

compare to other steering methods on In-distribution tasks? (RQ2): How does Cobras perform on OOD tasks? (RQ3): How does each parameter of Cobras impact its performance? 

Datasets. We conduct our evaluations on three prevalent steering datasets: TruthfulQA Lin et al. [2021] (TQA) for Truthfulness, Ultrafeedback Cui et al. [2023] (UF) for Helpfulness and RealToxici-tyPrompts Gehman et al. [2020] for Detoxification. Further, to compare the performance of steering methods on Out-of-Distribution (OOD) tasks, we use MMLU Hendrycks et al. [2020] and GSM8K Cobbe et al. [2021]. 

Baselines. Following ODESteer Zhao et al. [2026], we compare Cobras against the following baselines: Representation Engineering [Zou et al., 2023] (RepE), Inference-Time Intervention [Li et al., 2023] (ITI), Contrastive Activation Addition [Rimsky et al., 2024] (CAA), Minimally Modified Counterfactuals [Singh et al., 2024] (MiMiC), Linear Activation Transport [Rodriguez et al., 2025] (Linear-AcT), SphericalSteer-ing You et al. [2026] and ODESteer Zhao et al. [2026]. We test the steering methods on four open source models: Falcon-7B [Almazrouei et al., 2023], Mistral-7B [Jiang et al., 2023], LLaMA3.1-8B [Meta AI, 2024] and Qwen2.5-7B Team [2024]. 

Metrics. Following ODESteer Zhao et al. [2026], we use Truthfulness x Informativeness % as the primary metric for TQA while reporting Truthfulness% and Informativeness%. For UF/Helpfulness task, we use win rate over no steer (primary metric), Mean Reward and 90th Percentile Reward as secondary metrics. For Detoxification task, we use Toxicity Score from Perspective API Jigsaw and Google [2026] as primary metric while reporting Perplexity and Dist-2 as secondary metrics. For GSM8K and MMLU, we use accuracy as the metric. See Appendix C.3 for details. 

RQ1: How does Cobras compare to other steering methods on in-distribution tasks? We first evaluate Cobras in the standard in-distribution setting, where each steering method is trained and tested on the same task axis. We consider three representative alignment objectives: helpfulness on UltraFeedback, truthfulness on TruthfulQA, and detoxification on RealToxicityPrompts. 96 Conclusion 

By bridging the gap between heuristic methods and the rigorous optimization framework of the Schrödinger Bridge, this paper provides the first principled derivation of the log-density-ratio steering objective for activation steering. Experiments across four models and three alignment axes show that 

Cobras consistently outperforms baselines in-distribution while preserving OOD performance (commonly seen in fixed-direction steering methods), thanks to its query-adaptive nature. Limitations include the 

O(N+ · N−) cost of pairwise geodesic distances and Sinkhorn, which may be prohibitive for very large contrastive sets, and the reliance on a fixed spherical geometry that may not hold for all architectures or layers. Promising future directions include hierarchical transport solvers to reduce computational overhead and extending the framework to learned or more general Riemannian manifolds.

Specifically, we analyze how these seemingly disparate techniques implicitly optimize the ratio log (p+/p −) by reinterpreting them as specific geometric configurations of a continuous global flow. Formally, let p+ and p− define a pair of continuous densities corresponding to the contrastive behavior distributions D+ and D−, respectively. # C Experimental Details 

C.1 Base LLM Models 

Our base LLMs follow the experimental setup of Zhao et al. Zhao et al. [2026]. We describe them here for completeness. We evaluate all methods on four open-source models: (i) Falcon-7B, tiiuae/falcon-7b 2;(ii) Mistral-7B-v0.3, mistralai/Mistral-7B-v0.3 3; (iii) LLaMA3.1-8B, meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B 4;and (iv) Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B 5. We use the same generation configuration for all models and experiments: temperature = 0 .7, top-p = 0 .9, and repetition penalty = 1 .1.

C.2 Datasets 

• TruthfulQA [Lin et al., 2021] is a benchmark consisting of 817 questions paired with true and false reference answers, along with source information. The questions cover 38 diverse categories and are commonly evaluated in a zero-shot setting. Following Zhao et al. Zhao et al. [2026], we construct 5,918 question–answer pairs and use a 40/10/50 train/validation/test split with two-fold cross validation. 

• UltraFeedback [Cui et al., 2023] is a large-scale alignment dataset covering instruction-following qualities such as truthfulness, honesty, and helpfulness. In our experiments, we use the UltraFeedback-Binarized dataset 6. Specifically, we use the preference-modeling split, where each prompt is paired with an accepted response and a rejected response. Following Zhao et al. Zhao et al. [2026], we randomly sample 10K training pairs, 500 validation pairs, and 500 test pairs.

C.3 Metrics 

• Truthfulness (%). Truthfulness is evaluated on TruthfulQA Lin et al. [2021] using a publicly available fine-tuned LLaMA2-7B judge 8. The judge predicts a binary truthfulness label for each generated answer using prompts of the form: 

Q: {question} \n A: {answer}. \n True: 

We report the percentage of generations classified as truthful. 

• Informativeness (%). Informativeness is evaluated on TruthfulQA Lin et al. [2021] using a publicly available fine-tuned LLaMA2-7B judge 9. The judge predicts whether a response is informative or helpful using prompts of the form: 

Q: {question} \n A: {answer}. \n Helpful: 

We report the percentage of generations classified as informative. 

• Truthfulness × Informativeness (%). Following prior work, we report the percentage of responses that are both truthful and informative. For each prompt, this metric is computed as the product of the binary truthfulness and informativeness labels, and the final score is averaged over all prompts.
