# The Geometry Behind Diffusion and Flow Matching 

Gradient Flows and Geodesics in Wasserstein Space 

Yian Yao 

from the mortal world

Abstract 

The space P2(Rd) of probability measures with finite second moment carries a natural geometry: the quadratic Wasserstein distance W2 makes it a complete metric space and, following Otto, a (formal) Riemannian manifold whose geodesics are the optimal-transport interpolations. On this manifold, the gradient flow of the free energy F(ρ) = KL( ρ∥π) is exactly the Fokker–Planck equation, and its implicit-Euler discretization is the JKO scheme. This is the geometry underlying diffusion models: the forward process descends the free energy, and each denoising step realizes one JKO step, which recovers DDPM, DDIM, NCSN/SMLD, and Energy Matching—one scheme, not separate theories. The same manifold supports a second variational principle. Its geodesics—the minimum-action curves of the Benamou–Brenier formula—are precisely the optimal-transport paths that Flow Matching learns. Fixing both endpoints and following the geodesic, generation becomes a deterministic ODE along a straight line—hence far fewer sampling steps. Placing both families of models on one manifold makes their relationship exact: diffu-sion follows a free-energy gradient flow, an initial-value problem; optimal-transport Flow Matching follows a Wasserstein geodesic, a boundary-value problem. The two reach the same endpoints along different paths. Wasserstein distance endows geometry 

−−−−−−−−−−−→ probability space becomes a Riemannian manifold, carrying two complementary variational principles :

(A) Diffusion — free-energy gradient flow 

(initial-value problem: given ρk, descend F while staying close in W2)free energy → Fokker-Planck time discretization

(B) Flow Matching — Benamou–Brenier minimum action 

(boundary-value problem: given both ρ0 and ρ1, minimize kinetic energy) minimum action → Wasserstein geodesics → OT transport paths 

Essential difference: same endpoint ρ1 = pdata , different path.

# Outline 

• Prologue starts from the continuity and Fokker–Planck equations as they appear in diffusion models and Flow Matching, and isolates the connec-tions those treatments leave implicit. 

• Section 1 introduces the Wasserstein distance—the transport cost be-tween two distributions. 

• Section 2 derives the continuity equation—the local form of mass conser-vation. 

• Section 3 establishes the Riemannian structure: the Benamou–Brenier formula identifies W2 as a geodesic distance, and its geodesics as the optimal-transport paths learned by Flow Matching. 

• Section 4 derives the Fokker–Planck equation from a stochastic differen-tial equation and recasts it as a continuity equation. 

• Section 5 proves the central identity: the Fokker–Planck equation is the Wasserstein gradient flow of the free energy. 

• Section 6 discretizes the flow into the JKO scheme, proves convergence, and recovers DDPM, DDIM, NCSN/SMLD, Flow Matching, and Energy Matching from a single variational template.

The following notation is used frequently throughout this article and is collected here for easy reference. 

Sets and Spaces 

• Rd: d-dimensional Euclidean space. 

• Borel sets : Starting from all open sets in Rd and closing under countable unions, intersec-tions, and complements, one obtains the Borel σ-algebra , denoted B(Rd). Its elements are called Borel sets. Intuitively, all “reasonable” geometric sets (open, closed, countable unions/intersections) are Borel sets; virtually every set you encounter in practice is a Borel set. 

• P2(Rd): the set of all probability measures on Rd with finite second moment, i.e., probability measures μ satisfying R

Operators and Symbols 

• inf A (infimum ): the greatest lower bound of a set A. For example, inf {1/n : n ∈ N} = 0. Similar to min, but allows the minimum not to be attained (limit case). 

• sup A (supremum ): the least upper bound of a set A, a generalization of max. 

• arg min x f (x): the value of x at which f (x) attains its minimum (not the minimum value itself, but the “location” where the minimum is achieved). 

• ∇f : gradient (the vector of first partial derivatives of f with respect to spatial variables). 

• ∇ · v: divergence (the sum of partial derivatives of the components of a vector field v). 

• ∆f = ∇ · (∇f ): Laplacian. 

Key Terminology 

• a.e. (almost everywhere): a property “holds a.e.” means it may fail at some points, but the set of failure points has measure zero (“occupies zero volume”). For example, functions 

f and g are equal a.e. under Lebesgue measure means {x : f (x)̸ = g(x)} has zero volume. 

• Test function : a function in C∞ 

> c

The name comes from the fact that in the weak formulation of PDEs, we use them to “probe” whether the equation holds—multiply both sides of the PDE by a test function and integrate, converting the PDE into an integral 6identity that must hold for all test functions. This avoids requiring smoothness of the solution itself.

Remark 0.1 (Time-convention warning) . In Scenario 1 (diffusion), time runs from data (t = 0) to noise (t = T ). In Flow Matching below, the convention is reversed : t = 0 is noise and t = 1 is data . This article gives a unified, end-to-end account of diffusion and flow matching from a single geo-metric object: the Wasserstein manifold of probability measures. On it the two are not the same thing but complementary principles—diffusion is the gradient flow of the free energy (an initial-value problem), flow matching the geodesic, i.e. the minimal-action path (a boundary-value problem). Where most treatments proceed through stochastic processes and score functions, here one geometric idea is carried, without gaps, from the definition of the Wasserstein metric to the algorithms used in practice. Several lines of work reach this same picture from other directions; we place the article among them below. 

Unifying frameworks for flows and diffusions. The cleanest statement that diffu-sion and flow-based models are facets of one object is the stochastic interpolants framework of Albergo, Boffi & Vanden-Eijnden (2025): a single interpolant between two densities induces both a transport (continuity) equation and a family of forward/backward Fokker–Planck equa-tions with tunable noise, hence both deterministic (ODE) and stochastic (SDE) generators. Lipman et al. (2024), in the Flow Matching Guide and Code , give a comprehensive exposi-tion presenting diffusion paths as special cases of Flow Matching. Both unify the two through the SDE/ODE and velocity-field language; this article instead routes the unification through Wasserstein geometry—gradient flow versus geodesic. 

Diffusion as a Wasserstein gradient flow. Closest to the central identity of Section 5 is the work of Vuong, McCann, Santos & Lin (2025), who argue that diffusion training is better read as flow matching to the velocity field of a Wasserstein gradient flow than as score learning, and give numerical evidence that the learned field is generally not conservative (hence not a genuine score). 

JKO as a generative algorithm. A line of work builds generative models directly on the JKO scheme of Section 6: Mokrov et al. (2021) scale Wasserstein gradient flows using input-convex networks, and Xu, Cheng & Xie (2023, JKO-iFlow) stack residual blocks that each realize one JKO step. Energy Matching (Balcerak et al., 2025) and Equilibrium Matching (Wang et al., 2025) belong to the same family. 

A geometric caveat. The two principles should not be over-identified. Lavenant & San-tambrogio (2022) prove that the flow map of the Fokker–Planck equation is not the optimal-transport map: the diffusion trajectory is in general not the W2 geodesic between its endpoints. This is precisely the distinction drawn here—diffusion descends the free energy (an initial-value gradient flow, along a curved path), whereas OT Flow Matching follows the geodesic (a boundary-value problem)—and it is why the two reach the same endpoints by different paths.

Consider the following counterexample: 

μ = 0 .6 δa + 0 .4 δb, ν = 0 .5 δc + 0 .5 δd

If a map T satisfying T#μ = ν exists, then T can only send a to one of c or d, and likewise for b. But no assignment works: 

• T (a) = c, T (b) = d: then ν({c}) = μ(T −1({c})) = μ({a}) = 0 .6̸ = 0 .5 ✗

• T (a) = d, T (b) = c: then ν({d}) = μ({a}) = 0 .6̸ = 0 .5 ✗

• T (a) = c, T (b) = c: then ν({c}) = 0 .6 + 0 .4 = 1 ̸ = 0 .5 ✗

• All other cases fail similarly. 

Conclusion: No transport map from μ to ν exists. 

An optimal coupling , however, does exist. The optimal plan is: split 0.5 from a to c, send the remaining 0.1 to d; send all 0.4 from b to d:

γ = 0 .5 δ(a,c ) + 0 .1 δ(a,d ) + 0 .4 δ(b,d )

Verification of marginals: the total mass of γ on a is 0 .5 + 0 .1 = 0 .6 = μ({a}) ✓; the total mass of γ on c is 0 .5 = ν({c}) ✓; and so on. 

This is why Brenier’s theorem requires the condition “ μ is absolutely continu-ous”: 

• Discrete distributions (as above): a finite amount of mass is concentrated at a single point, and transport may necessarily require splitting , so a map T is insufficient—one must use a coupling γ.

• Absolutely continuous distributions (with density ρ): each point carries only an “infinitesimal” mass ρ(x)dx and never needs to be split—even if ν is discrete, no source point has “a bulk of mass that must be allocated to different destinations” (since each point of μ has zero mass). Therefore a deterministic map T suffices. Note: only μ needs to be absolutely continuous; there is no restriction on ν.

In one sentence: Absolutely continuous ⇒ mass is “continuously spread” ⇒ no splitting needed ⇒ map T exists ⇒ Brenier applies. Discrete/singular distribution ⇒ there are “bulk” masses ⇒ splitting may be necessary ⇒

only a coupling γ can describe the solution ⇒ Brenier does not apply.

4.1 Overview 

We begin with a microscopic stochastic process—particles undergoing Brownian motion in a potential field—and ask how the macroscopic probability density of the particles evolves. The answer is the Fokker–Planck equation, a partial differential equation governing the density over time. The goals of this section: 1. Explain the physical meaning of SDEs (stochastic differential equations). 2. Derive the Fokker–Planck equation from SDEs (with detailed explanation of each step). 3. Rewrite the Fokker–Planck equation in the form of a continuity equation (preparing the connection to gradient flows).

4.9 Where is the modeling choice in diffusion? 

The hand-crafted part of diffusion models is not the Itˆ o rule itself. Once Brownian motion is chosen, the identity ( dB t)2 = dt is a mathematical consequence of its quadratic variation. The modeling choice is the SDE: 

dX t = f (Xt, t ) dt + g(t) dB t.

Choosing the drift f , the diffusion strength g, and the noise schedule (for example the β(t)schedule in VP-SDE) specifies the forward noising process. The Fokker–Planck equation is then the deterministic density evolution implied by that SDE. Flow Matching removes this SDE modeling layer. It does not first choose Brownian noise and then derive a Fokker–Planck equation. Instead, it directly learns a velocity field vt for a chosen probability path, often an OT-like path from noise to data.

6.5 Rigorous convergence theorem 

Theorem 6.2 (JKO, 1998; Ambrosio–Gigli–Savar´ e, 2008) . Assume V is λ-convex ( ∇2V ≥ λI ,

Four key steps of the proof: 

1. Existence : Each JKO subproblem has a solution—the W 22 term provides compactness, and F is lower semicontinuous in the W2 topology. 2. Energy decay : F(ρτk+1 ) ≤ F (ρτk), since ρk itself is a feasible solution (with cost F(ρk) + 0). 3. Uniform estimates : Energy decay + control of the Fisher information yields compact-ness of {ρτk}, enabling passage to the limit τ → 0. 4. with respect to another measure λ (written μ ≪ λ) if: 

λ(A) = 0 = ⇒ μ(A) = 0 for all measurable sets A

In words: every set that is “invisible” to λ is also “invisible” to μ.

In plain language: If a set has “zero volume” ( λ(A) = 0), then μ also places no mass on it ( μ(A) = 0). 

Intuition 

Everyday analogy: Spreading butter vs. placing pebbles 

Imagine distributing something on a slice of bread ( Rd space). There are two ways: 

• Spreading butter (absolutely continuous): Butter is spread uniformly or non-uniformly on the bread surface. If you cut along a line of zero area, there will be no butter on it—because butter is “continuously spread,” and no area means no butter. 

• Placing pebbles (not absolutely continuous): You place several pebbles on the bread. The pebbles occupy “zero area” (points have no area), yet they have finite mass. This violates absolute continuity—there exists a set of zero area (the points where the pebbles sit), yet it carries positive mass. 

The essence of absolute continuity is: mass is spread like butter across space, not piled up like pebbles at isolated points. 

A finer understanding: What are “sets of zero volume”? 

In spaces of different dimensions, “zero volume” means different things: 

• In R1 (one-dimensional/line): Points, countable sets (e.g., the rationals Q), and the Cantor set are all null sets 

• In R2 (two-dimensional/plane): Points, line segments, and curves are all null sets (they have no “area”) 

• In R3 (three-dimensional/space): Points, line segments, and surfaces are all null sets (they have no “volume”) General rule: In Rd, subsets of dimension < d are typically null. 

Key corollary: An absolutely continuous measure μ on Rd satisfies μ({x}) = 0 for all individual points x. That is, the mass at any single point is zero —there are no “atoms.” Mass must be “spread out” over regions with positive volume. 66 Theorem A.3 (Radon–Nikodym) . μ ≪ λ if and only if there exists a non-negative measurable function ρ : Rd → [0 , ∞) such that:

Why does optimal transport care about absolute continuity? 

Recall the Brenier theorem: If μ is absolutely continuous (has a density), then the optimal transport map T exists and is unique. Why is this condition needed? The intuition is as follows: 

Core idea: “Infinitesimal mass does not need to be split” 

A map T requires that all mass at position x goes to the same destination T (x). 

• If μ has an “atom” (finite mass μ({x0}) > 0 at some point x0), and ν requires this mass to be distributed to multiple locations, then a map T cannot achieve this— since T (x0)can only be a single point. 

• But if μ is absolutely continuous, then every point x carries only “infinitesimal” mass 

ρ(x)dx . This infinitesimal mass going entirely to one destination T (x) is perfectly fine— since each portion of mass from the source is infinitesimal, there is no need to “split” it for the map T to transport it to a unique destination. The target ν is formed by “accu-mulating” infinitesimal masses sent from many different x. Note: ν itself need not be absolutely continuous—the Brenier theorem only requires μ to be absolutely continuous. 

Analogy: Distributing fruit 

• 10 apples (discrete/atomic) must be divided among 3 people, each requiring 3.33— cannot divide into integers , must cut (split). 67 • 10 liters of juice (continuous/absolutely continuous) must be divided among 3 people, each requiring 3.33 liters— easy to divide , liquid can be poured to arbitrary precision. An absolutely continuous measure is like a liquid: it can be “poured” by the map T to any destination without needing to be “cut.”

• Independent coupling: γ = μ ⊗ ν (X and Y are independent) 

• Perfect correlation: γ = (id , T )#μ (Y = T (X) is deterministically determined by X)

• Various intermediate cases The set of couplings Γ( μ, ν ) encompasses all these possibilities. 

Level three: The transport plan perspective 

Returning to the earth-moving metaphor. γ(x, y ) represents “the amount of earth moved from position x to position y.” The marginal conditions guarantee: 

• The total amount moved from x = the amount of earth at μ(x) (all earth at x is moved away) 

• The total amount arriving at y = the amount needed at ν(y) (all demand at y is satisfied)

Key Insight 

Coupling = encoding a transport plan via a joint distribution 

This is the most crucial intuition for understanding optimal transport. A joint distribu-tion γ(x, y ) simultaneously tells you two things: 1. Who is paired with whom: The larger the “density” of γ at ( x, y ), the more mass is transported from x to y

2. The pairing satisfies supply-demand balance: The marginal conditions ensure every source point’s mass is fully allocated, and every target point’s demand is fully met Different joint distributions = different transport strategies: 

• γ concentrated on the diagonal x = y ⇒ “stay in place” 71 • γ concentrated on some curve y = T (x) ⇒ “deterministic transport” (Monge map)

x is dispersed to multiple y)A joint distribution is an extremely flexible representation—it can express determin-istic maps ( γ degenerates onto the graph of the map), as well as “one-to-many” mass splitting ( γ spreads in the y direction). Key Insight 

The essence of coupling: 

Given two distributions μ and ν, a coupling γ describes one specific plan for “who goes where.” The same pair ( μ, ν ) admits infinitely many couplings (transport plans), each with different cost. Optimal transport finds the coupling with minimum cost. The 

Wasserstein distance is that minimum cost (square root). 

Why use couplings instead of maps T directly? 

Monge’s original idea was to find a map T such that T#μ = ν. But a map requires “one-to-one”—all mass at x must go to a single destination. If μ = δ0 and ν = 12 δ−1 + 12 δ1 (one point splits into two), no such map exists! Kantorovich’s coupling relaxes this restriction: the mass at a point x can be split among multiple destinations. This ensures: (1) the optimization problem always has a solution; (2) the problem becomes linear programming , which is easier to handle.

D.1 Core intuition 

At thermal equilibrium, low-energy states occur with exponentially higher probability than high-energy states. The probability ratio between two states depends only on their energy difference: π(x1)

D.2 The role of temperature T —the single control knob 

• T → 0 ( β → ∞ ): the distribution degenerates to a Dirac delta; the system “freezes” at the lowest-energy state 

• T → ∞ (β → 0): the distribution approaches uniform; the system completely ignores energy and wanders randomly 84 • Finite T : a compromise between the two—low-energy states are “preferred” but not exclusively Intuition: temperature controls the ratio of “exploration” to “exploitation.” Low tempera-ture = pure exploitation (stay at the optimum); high temperature = pure exploration (wander everywhere).

H.1 Langevin dynamics 

Langevin dynamics is a stochastic differential equation originally proposed by Paul Langevin in 1908 (Langevin, 1908) to describe the Brownian motion of particles in a fluid. In modern probability, statistical mechanics, and machine learning, the most common form is the over-damped Langevin dynamics :

dX t = −∇ V (Xt) dt + √2ε dB t

where V is the potential energy function, ε > 0 is the temperature/noise strength, and Bt is standard Brownian motion. In the main text we often set ε = 1 for simplicity. 

Physical intuition: A particle moves in the potential energy landscape V , subject to two effects: 

• −∇ V : deterministic drift, pushing the particle toward regions of low potential energy. 

• √2ε dB t: random thermal noise, preventing all particles from collapsing to a single mini-mizer of V .

Stationary distribution. The probability density ρt of Xt satisfies the Fokker–Planck equation 

∂tρt = ∇ · (ρt∇V ) + ε∆ρt.

At stationarity, the deterministic force and the entropic force balance: 

−∇ V − ε∇ log ρ∞ = 0 .

Thus Langevin dynamics samples from the Gibbs/Boltzmann distribution with temperature ε.

In machine learning.

H.8 KKT conditions 

The KKT conditions (Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions) are necessary optimality conditions for constrained optimization problems, generalizing the “gradient equals zero” condition from unconstrained optimization. Consider the problem: min f (x) subject to g(x) = 0. The Lagrangian is L(x, λ ) = f (x) + 

λ g (x). The KKT conditions require: 

∇xL = ∇f (x) + λ ∇g(x) = 0 , g(x) = 0 

Intuition: On the constraint surface, the gradient of the objective function must be parallel to the normal direction of the constraint surface (otherwise one could continue descending along the constraint surface). The multiplier λ measures “how tight” the constraint is.
