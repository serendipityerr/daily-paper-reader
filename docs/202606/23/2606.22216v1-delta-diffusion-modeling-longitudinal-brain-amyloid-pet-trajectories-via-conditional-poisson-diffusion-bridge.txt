# ∆-Diffusion: Modeling Longitudinal Brain Amyloid-PET Trajectories via Conditional Poisson Diffusion Bridge 

Yongheng Sun, Mengqi Wu, Minhui Yu, Maureen Kohi, Mingxia Liu 

University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill 

mingxia liu@med.unc.edu 

Abstract. While longitudinal brain PET imaging is the gold stan-dard for quantifying the spatiotemporal accumulation of β-amyloid, its widespread clinical utility is constrained by high operational costs and cumulative radiation risks. Recent deep generative models show promise in longitudinal image synthesis; however, they often fail to capture sub-tle pathological progression due to identity drift and a persistent bias toward trivially replicating baseline signal intensities rather than model-ing temporal transition. To this end, we propose ∆-Diffusion , a novel progression-aware framework that redefines longitudinal PET synthesis as a conditional Poisson Diffusion Bridge (PDB) process. Unlike standard diffusion models that start from Gaussian noise, our PDB formulation is mathematically anchored to the subject’s baseline PET, effectively trans-forming the generative task into a conditional distribution transition of the amyloid trajectory. To handle heteroscedastic nature of PET imag-ing, we introduce a physically-grounded Poisson perturbation within a Diffusion Transformer (DiT). This architecture uses adaptive scale-shift modulation to precisely calibrate the synthesis with the elapsed clini-cal interval and structural MRI context. A volume-of-interest balanced objective is designed to emphasize sparse, high-risk regions of amyloid accumulation. Validated on two cohorts with 542 subjects, ∆-Diffusion demonstrates superior performance in capturing longitudinal variations in amyloid deposition compared to state-of-the-art methods, offering a robust computational framework for tracking disease progression. 

Keywords: Longitudinal Synthesis · Brain Amyloid-PET · Diffusion.

There is a critical need for computational methods capable of forecasting future PET states from a single baseline session, providing a non-invasive way to impute follow-up data or predict disease staging. Despite recent success of deep learning in medical image synthesis [1], lon-gitudinal PET forecasting presents unique challenges that standard static PET synthesis models fail to address. First , because follow-up scans are anchored to the same subject, models often suffer from spatial-identity drift [7] or, conversely, a tendency to trivially copy the baseline scan without capturing progression [4]. 

Second , clinical follow-up intervals are highly heterogeneous; ignoring the elapsed time leads to temporally miscalibrated results. Third , the signal-dependent na-ture of PET noise (governed by Poisson statistics) is poorly modeled by Gaussian diffusion. This necessitates a physically-grounded model to preserve critical sig-nals in low-SNR regions inherent to PET’s photon-counting physics. To address these limitations, we propose a progression-aware ∆-Diffusion 

framework. Unlike traditional generative models that treat the baseline as a sec-ondary condition, our method reformulates longitudinal synthesis as a Poisson Diffusion Bridge (PDB) process. As shown in Fig. 1(a), we construct a diffusion bridge that is mathematically anchored to the subject’s baseline PET. This for-mulation redefines the generative objective as learning the conditional distribu-tion transition , effectively mitigating the trivial identity task and ensuring strict Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 3

subject-specific consistency. To improve physiological fidelity, we incorporate a physically-grounded Poisson perturbation layer and a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) that explicitly calibrates the output based on the follow-up interval and anatomical context from T1-weighted (T1w) MRI. We also design a volume-of-interest (VOI)-balanced objective to prioritize learning in clinically significant regions. Experiments on two cohorts ( N =542) with longitudinal PIB- and AV45-PET scans demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches in predicting longitudinal amyloid variations.

Conditioning. We parameterize sθ with a DiT (comprising K=28 blocks, fol-lowing [15]). To incorporate temporal guidance, we inject (i) diffusion time step

This design is aligned with adaptive layer normalization used in volumetric transformers. For structural guidance, the baseline T1w MRI xM is integrated as a conditional input to provide anatomy priors, while the bridge endpoint xPT anchors the subject-specific baseline. 

2.2 Physically-Grounded Poisson Perturbation 

Standard diffusion/bridge constructions often rely on Gaussian perturbations. However, PET noise is dominated by Poisson counting statistics [19]. To address this physical characteristic, we replace the forward perturbation with a Poisson corruption operator. Let zt denote the perturbed sample at diffusion step t ∈{0, · · · , T }. Mathematically, Poisson diffusion can be written as:         

> zt= perturb( zt−1, λ t),λ1< λ 2<· · · < λ T,(6)

where λt controls the noise intensity and is typically increased with t (e.g., lin-early interpolated in [0 , 1]). Physically-grounded Poisson perturbation can be im-plemented by applying Poisson deviates in the projection (sinogram) domain [9]. The sinogram is generated via a Radon transform to emulate acquisition-related photon noise, followed by an inverse reconstruction to obtain zt.Although image-domain noise from Poisson sinogram perturbations is not strictly i.i.d. Gaussian, it can be approximated as heteroscedastic Gaussian un-der moderate-to-high count regimes [2]. This justifies adopting the standard scheduling used in [25] as a practical approximation, while keeping the forward process physically grounded in Poisson counting statistics. In our framework, Poisson perturbation is applied in the forward process to generate realistic in-termediate states and enhance the robustness of the reverse denoising trajectory.

## 3 Experiment 

Materials and Image Preprocessing. Two datasets are used. (1) OASIS-3 [11]: 283 subjects have paired baseline PIB-PET and T1w MRI, with 1–4 follow-up PIB-PET sessions, yielding 653 longitudinal pairs. These samples were split into training/validation (463/51 pairs, 226 subjects) and test (139 pairs, 57 subjects) sets. (2) ADNI [8]: 259 subjects have paired baseline AV45-PET and T1w MRI, with 1–5 follow-up AV45-PET sessions, yielding 835 longitudi-nal pairs. These samples were split into training/validation (614/68 pairs, 207 subjects) and test (153 pairs, 52 subjects) sets. Data splits were performed at the subject level to avoid data leakage, ensuring that no subject appears in more than one set. For MRIs, the preprocessing pipeline includes skull stripping, in-tensity normalization, and non-linear registration to the MNI space. PET scans are first skull-stripped and linearly aligned to their respective MRIs; they are then warped to the MNI space using MRI-derived deformation fields. To remove non-informative background voxels, all volumes are uniformly cropped to dimen-sions of 160 ×180 ×160. Experimental Settings. We evaluate PET generation using both whole-image 

and region-level metrics: PSNR and SSIM over the full 3D volume for measuring global reconstruction quality, and within the cortical VOI [10] to measure accu-racy of localized tracer uptake. We compare ∆-Diffusion with SOTA generative methods: a hybrid adversarial model ( VAEGAN [13]), three standard diffusion frameworks ( DDPM [5], DDIM [22], and PNDM [16]), and a latent diffu-sion model ( LDM [18]). We also compare our method with ControlNet [24] for structure-conditioned synthesis, as well as FICD [23] and SiM2P [15] for medical image translation. For all the competing methods, we utilize the same training/validation/test splits and preprocessing pipeline to ensure a fair com-parison. All methods are trained on a cluster with NVIDIA H100 GPUs, using the Adam optimizer (learning rate: 1 ×10 −4, batch size: 1). 

Quantitative Results. Table 1 and Table 2 report the quantitative perfor-mance of various methods for longitudinal PIB-PET and AV45-PET synthesis. Our ∆-Diffusion consistently outperforms all competing methods, achieving the highest PSNR and SSIM across both datasets. In terms of global reconstruction 

quality, our ∆-Diffusion improves PSNR by approximately 3.54 dB on OASIS-3 (28.6489 vs. 25.1127 for SiM2P) and 0.62 dB on ADNI, demonstrating supe-rior anatomical fidelity. Furthermore, our method achieves the highest accuracy in localized tracer uptake , as evidenced by the superior metrics recorded within VOIs. While advanced diffusion-based models like LDM and SiM2P generally offer improved stability over standard voxel-space diffusion ( e.g. , DDPM), they still exhibit significantly lower structural similarity and higher intensity errors compared to our framework. Paired t-tests on whole-image PSNR show that 

∆-Diffusion significantly outperforms the SOTA methods ( p < 0.05).

Ablation Study. We conduct an ablation study on ∆-Diffusion using OASIS-3 data, evaluating performance under five settings: (1) w/o PMVD : the base-line configuration using Gaussian noise (instead of Poisson), removing all condi-tioning inputs (MRI and ∆d ) and replacing the VOI-balanced objective with a whole-image MSE loss; (2) w/o MVD : using Poisson noise and the whole-image MSE loss, but excluding MRI and ∆d ; (3) w/o MD : removing MRI and ∆d ;(4) w/o VD : using the whole-image MSE loss and removing ∆d ; (5) w/o MV :removing MRI and using the whole-image MSE loss. The quantitative results in Table 3 suggest that the full ∆-Diffusion model achieves the best performance across almost all metrics. The most significant performance drop is observed in the w/o PMVD variant (PSNR: 25.1127 vs. 28.6489), highlighting that the combination of Poisson perturbation and structural/temporal guidance is crit-ical for accurate longitudinal synthesis. Comparing w/o MV to the full model shows that the inclusion of structural MRI and VOI-balanced objective further refines global structural similarity and localized region-level accuracy. ## 4 Conclusion 

This paper presents ∆-Diffusion, a progression-aware diffusion framework for longitudinal synthesis of amyloid PET. By conditioning on MRI and elapsed follow-up interval, our model learns distributional transitions from baseline to follow-up PET via a Poisson Diffusion Bridge, rather than a baseline-conditioned noise-to-image generation process. This approach suppresses trivial copying of baseline features and ensures both anatomical consistency and accurate temporal calibration. Extensive experiments on two cohorts with longitudinal PIB- and AV45-PET suggest that ∆-Diffusion consistently outperforms SOTA methods. Title Suppressed Due to Excessive Length 9
