## Abstract 

We propose Acc-Sinkhorn, a simple accelerated variant of Sinkhorn for entropy-regularized optimal transport (EOT). The method is derived from a bilevel optimiza-tion view: Sinkhorn row scaling solves the inner variable u exactly and defines the reduced dual objective f (v) = min u F (u, v ), while the remaining column scaling is a unit-step dual mirror descent step in v. This structure yields a Hessian-driven Nesterov acceleration that keeps Sinkhorn’s scaling form and per-iteration cost, using only extrapolated combinations of Sinkhorn iterates. We prove an O(1 /k 2)

rate under a verifiable stability condition. For an ε-approximation of unregularized OT, the resulting complexity is eO(n2/ε ), improved from eO(n2/ε 2) for Sinkhorn. On synthetic problems, color transfer, and word alignment, Acc-Sinkhorn gives a 

10 ×–30 × speedup over Sinkhorn at small regularization.

Sinkhorn algorithm. The Sinkhorn algorithm [ 29 ] alternately rescales the rows and columns of the kernel matrix K = exp( −C/ε ) to enforce the marginal constraints. Each iteration costs O(nm )

and is easy to parallelize. Its convergence has been studied through Hilbert’s projective metric, block coordinate descent, sharp ε-dependent estimates, and mirror descent in continuous and discrete time [17, 5, 20, 10, 27, 24, 21, 3, 11]. Despite these advances, a central question remains: can Sinkhorn be accelerated to improve its accuracy dependence without losing its simplicity? 

Related Work. We call a method accelerated if its error decays as O(1 /k 2) rather than the O(1 /k )

rate of Sinkhorn, where k is the number of full Sinkhorn-type iterations. For simplicity, we discuss complexity for m = n. For an ε-approximation of the original OT problem, the total cost is often 

O(np/ε q ) for positive p and q. Existing acceleration methods fall into three broad categories. 

(i) Coordinate-wise and stochastic methods. Coordinate-wise and stochastic variants, such as Greenkhorn, reduce the cost of each iteration by updating only part of the variables or by using stochastic estimates [ 1, 22 , 18 ]. These methods can improve practical efficiency, but they retain the 

O(1 /k ) convergence behavior of Sinkhorn and thus do not improve its dependence on ε.

(ii) Nesterov acceleration and primal-dual methods. Accelerated primal-dual methods, such as APDAGD, improve the ε-dependence of Sinkhorn by applying Nesterov-type acceleration or accelerated mirror descent to the EOT dual problem [ 15 , 22 ]. However, they typically require line search or adaptation to local smoothness and are more complex to implement than Sinkhorn. 

(iii) Sinkhorn variants with modified update rules. Sinkhorn variants with modified update rules, such as overrelaxed Sinkhorn and annealed Sinkhorn, keep the scaling structure of Sinkhorn while changing the update or regularization schedule [ 31 , 8]. They can improve local convergence or practical behavior, but they do not give a global O(1 /k 2) acceleration with a parameter-free update. In short, existing methods do not simultaneously achieve an accelerated O(1 /k 2) rate, O(n2/ε )

complexity, and a parameter-free simple implementation; see Table 1. Table 1: Comparison of algorithms for entropy-regularized optimal transport ( n = m).

Our contribution. The main contribution of this paper is Acc-Sinkhorn , a simple accelerated variant of Sinkhorn that keeps the O(nm ) per-iteration cost of Sinkhorn while improving the convergence rate from O(1 /k ) to O(1 /k 2). As shown in Table 1, Acc-Sinkhorn is the only method in the table that combines accelerated convergence, Sinkhorn-level per-iteration cost, and a simple update rule without line search. Its smoothness parameter is fixed by the geometry, namely L = 1 , and the strong-convexity parameter μ is handled by a homotopy schedule rather than by manual tuning. Numerically, Acc-Sinkhorn achieves a 10 ×–30 × speedup over Sinkhorn on synthetic datasets, color transfer, and word alignment at small ε.The acceleration is built on a bilevel view of Sinkhorn. ## 6 Conclusion 

This paper presents a simple and efficient accelerated Sinkhorn algorithm for computing entropy-regularized optimal transport that maintains the per-iteration cost of Sinkhorn while achieving a 

10 ×–30 × speedup in the small ε regime. Lyapunov convergence analysis proves the O(1 /k 2) rate under a verifiable stability condition and suggests the overall complexity eO(n2/ε ), compared to 

O(1 /k ) and eO(n2/ε 2) for Sinkhorn. Numerical experiments on synthetic and real datasets support the practical efficiency of the method. Removing the stability condition and proving the limit step size α = √2μ directly remain important directions for future work. 9References
