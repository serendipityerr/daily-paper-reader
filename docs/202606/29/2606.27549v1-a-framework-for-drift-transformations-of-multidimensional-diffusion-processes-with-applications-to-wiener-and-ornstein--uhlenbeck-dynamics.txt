# A framework for drift transformations of multidimensional diffusion processes with applications to Wiener and Ornstein–Uhlenbeck dynamics 

Antonio Di Crescenzo ∗ Verdiana Mustaro † Serena Spina ‡

Dipartimento di Matematica, Università degli Studi di Salerno, Via Giovanni Paolo II, 132, 84084 Fisciano (SA), Italy 

(paper submitted to international journal on November 9, 2025) 

Abstract 

We investigate a class of drift-based transformations between multidimensional diffusion pro-cesses. The approach allows to construct a new process whose transition probability density function (p.d.f.) can be expressed in a product form involving the p.d.f. of the original diffusion. The frame-work is formulated in terms of stochastic differential equations providing general conditions under which the transformed p.d.f. remains analytically tractable in the multidimensional setting. The trans-formation is defined through a weight function 𝑤 , derived as the solution of a suitable partial differ-ential equation. Also, specific forms of 𝑤 yield certain mixture representations of the transformed p.d.f., which also leads to identify a bimodal feature. We establish closed-form relations between the original and transformed diffusions focusing on features such as stochastic ordering, Poissonian resetting, and diffusions in potential fields. The analysis of the usual stochastic order highlights how the transformation can substantially alter the probabilistic behavior of the process. Furthermore, the product-form relation is shown to persist under Poisson-paced resets, allowing for explicit stationary distributions in certain cases. Two fundamental case studies are presented, based on transforma-tions of the Wiener and Ornstein–Uhlenbeck processes, for which explicit expressions of the weight function, potential function, and transition densities are derived. Special attention is given to the two-dimensional setting, where symmetry properties and absorbing boundaries are explored, providing further insight into the structure and behavior of the transformed diffusions.

# 1 Introduction and background 

Transition probability density functions (p.d.f.’s) of multidimensional diffusion processes play a cru-cial role in the stochastic modeling of dynamic behavior in various real-life systems. However, explicit closed-form expressions for transition p.d.f.’s in dimension 𝑛 ≥ 1 are generally unavailable, except in a few special cases. Our goal is to exploit a method for generating multidimensional transition p.d.f.’s and to explore the properties and interrelationships between the resulting densities. 

1.1 Recent advances on multidimensional diffusion processes 

Beyond the present work, several recent studies have further advanced the understanding of multidimen-sional diffusion processes, addressing different aspects and applications. Hereafter, we recall a selection of works on theoretical, methodological, and applied perspectives of these processes. 2Diffusion processes provide a suitable framework for capturing the complexity of systems among various applied contexts, such as neural activity (see Iyengar [24]) and geophysics systems (cf. Xiao et al. [46]). We point out that they are also employed in emerging frameworks, such as graphical applications related to the use of artificial intelligence (see Wang et al. [45], for instance). The theory related to diffusion processes deals with various aspects. For instance, in Trevisan [43], the author investigates the well-posedness for martingale solutions of stochastic differential equations, giving a very general equivalence between different descriptions for multidimensional diffusion processes under minimal regularity and integrability assumptions. The martingale approach is also used by Chen and Li [4] to construct coupling methods that yield upper bound estimates in two different probability metrics for diffusion processes. The paper by Herrmann et al. [22] studies dynamical systems with two meta-stable states under slow periodic and small random perturbations, modeled as weakly time-inhomogeneous diffusions. The study extends previous results to higher dimensions, employing large deviation theory to analyze exit times and transition probabilities. In Kuosoka et al. [27], the authors study multidimensional diffusion processes in random environments defined by 𝛼 -stable Lévy processes, establishing conditions that determine whether these diffusions are recurrent or transient. Moreover, Boege et al. [1] analyze 𝑛 -dimensional stationary diffusion processes iwhose drift is defined by a Lipschitz-continuous function. Specifically, they study the conditional independence relations of the distributions when the drift function is assumed to be sparse. A particular interest arises for diffusions in bounded domains. Grigorescu and Kang [21], for instance, study a Brownian motion in a bounded domain that restarts at a fixed point when hitting the boundary. The stationary distribution, the resolvent, and the proof of the exponential ergodicity are given by using two generalizable methods. Del Moral and Villemonais [7] analyze a multidimensional diffusion process in a bounded domain, where particles can be "killed" either by hitting the boundary or at random times. Using a branching particle model, they study the behavior of particles that avoid being killed. In the recent literature, a great interest is also given to the parameters’ estimation of multidimensional processes. Oga and Koike [37] study a deep neural network method to estimate the drift coefficient of a multidimensional diffusion process from discrete observations. The problem of parametric inference for multidimensional diffusions is faced also in the work by Gobet and Stazhynski [19], based on observa-tions at random stopping times. Jacod [25] is interested in estimating the drift and diffusion coefficients under suitable smoothness and identifiability conditions, without any kind of ergodicity or even recur-rence assumption on the diffusion process. Moreover, Li [30] proposes a widely applicable method of approximate maximum-likelihood estimation for multivariate diffusion process from discretely sampled data. 

1.2 Aims of the paper 

This paper aims to develop a method for the generation of new p.d.f.’s for multidimensional diffusion processes by means of a transformation which only modifies the drift values. We take into account an analogous method for one-dimensional diffusions (see Giorno and Nobile [16] and references therein). The approach relies on a product-form relation between the transition p.d.f.’s of non-singular diffusion processes in ℝ𝑛 (𝑛 > 1) , by means of an auxiliary weight function 𝑤 . The issue of transformations for dif-fusion dynamics has undoubtedly been explored in the literature. Indeed, several alternative transforma-tion schemes for diffusion processes have been proposed in the recent years, both in the one-dimensional and multidimensional settings. See, for instance, Giorno et al. [17] and references therein, in which the authors derive first-passage-time p.d.f.’s for one-dimensional time-homogeneous diffusion processes re-stricted between constant boundaries by making use of monotone transformation functions. Furthermore, in the context of multidimensional transformations, Forman and Sørensen [14] propose transformations 3of well-known diffusion models, such as the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) and Pearson diffusions, that pre-serve key properties of the original processes, including their mixing rates and first-passage time dis-tributions. These transformations yield flexible and statistically tractable multi-modal diffusion models with easily estimable parameters, which the authors apply to molecular dynamics data. A noteworthy feature of the method presented in the following is that it yields results not accessible through standard general techniques, such as space-time transformations (see, for instance, Ricciardi [40] for a trasformation of one-dimensional diffusions, or Nagasawa [35] for harmonic space-time trans-formations of multi-dimensional processes). We show that applying the proposed methodology to the multidimensional case still yields a well-posed transformed diffusion process, although, unlike in the one-dimensional setting, a general expression for the weight function 𝑤 cannot be obtained. Nevertheless, this is a quite interesting outcome, as it allows to define different product transformations, leading to a family of new diffusion processes, as long as the conditions on the function 𝑤 are met. It is worth noting that the initial process on which the transfor-mation is performed must have at least one attractive endpoint in order to derive suitable expressions for the weight function. Furthermore, we obtain the general expression of the infinitesimal moments for the new processes and observe that they can always be represented as a well-posed mixture involving the initial process. After presenting the main theorem for constructing the transition p.d.f. of the transformed process, we focus on two specific cases, where we aim to obtain transformations of well-known diffusions, namely the Wiener and OU processes in ℝ𝑛 . In the first case, the resulting expression for the transition p.d.f. is noteworthy, since the transformed diffusion can be seen as a mixture of two Wiener processes. Con-sequently, we conclude that transforming a Wiener process through an appropriate weight function 𝑤 

yileds a diffusion that is at most bimodal, and exhibits two peaks of equal height for a specific choice of the parameter appearing in 𝑤 . The emergence of a transformed process that can display bimodality is particularly interesting. Indeed, diffusion models exhibiting multimodality are useful in the description of clustering phenomena and in identifying centroids or distinct dynamical regimes in complex systems. Such structures provide a probabilistic framework for modeling clustered behaviors that naturally arise in various applied contexts, including those analyzed through machine learning techniques (see, e.g., Celletti [3]). We also point out that mixtures of measures of Wiener processes are useful to detect the presence of a random drift (see Johnson et al. [26]). The diffusion processes obtained through this approach exhibit remarkable properties that make them particularly useful for applications in different fields. Specifically, the spatial transformation introduced here induces a transport of probability mass between specific regions of the 𝑛 -dimensional domain on which the original and the transformed processes are defined. This is useful for the analysis of maximal couplings, as well as for the construction of upper bounds for the Wasserstein distance between the prob-ability measures of the two processes, which can be exploited in several applied contexts (see Lavenant et al. [28] for the use of bimodal densities in the context of optimal transport). Once the product-form transformation has been specified, we also aim to apply it in the construction of new diffusion processes whose motion is subject to Poissonian resetting. Such topic has already been treated in several contributions. For instance, Dharmaraja et al. [8] focuses on an Ehrenfest model (whose jump-diffusion approximation leads to the OU process) subject to resets to the origin governed by a time-homogeneous Poisson process. Also, Magdziarz and Taźbierski [33] derive some properties of Brownian motion with Poissonian resetting by making use of a general stochastic representation. Another aspect we aim to investigate for the considered diffusion processes concens the first-passage time through a barrier described by a known regular function. In particular, we observe throughout this work that the employed transformation and a fruitful symmetry-based approach allow us to obtain a 4closed-form expression for the taboo transition p.d.f. of the modified diffusion. We emphasize that such an explicit result is not guaranteed, since many studies on crossing times for diffusion processes involve only numerical computations or asymptotic approximations.

This paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the main theoretical results about the product-form relationship between the transition p.d.f.’s of non-singular diffusion processes in ℝ𝑛 (𝑛 > 1) . Special attention is paid to the conditions on the auxiliary weight function 𝑤 that correctly define the transformed diffusion. Moreover, general properties of the transformed process and the weight function are discussed. The analysis also includes comparisons of the diffusion processes through the usual stochastic order, the application to processes subject to resets, and a representation based on diffusion in a potential. Two case studies starting from well-known processes are presented in Section 3. Specifically, Section 3.1 details the main results concerning the transformation of an 𝑛 -dimensional Wiener process, where a suitable weight function, based on an exponential function, is considered along with the infinitesimal moments and the potential function of the resulting transformed process. It should be noted that the transition p.d.f. of the transformed process is expressed as a 2-mixture of Gaussian densities. A similar discussion is presented in Section 3.2 for a transformed Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, where the weight function is instead based on the error function. Section 4 is devoted to a special case about the transformation of a symmetric two-dimensional process in the presence of an absorbing boundary. An expression of the taboo transition p.d.f. of the transformed process is given in terms of the transition p.d.f. of the original process. Section 5 focuses on the transformation of a two-dimensional linear process, and gives a general expression of the weight function. Moreover, in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 we give a specific view of the two-dimensional transformed Wiener and OU processes, respectively. In Section 6, the work is completed with some concluding remarks and prompts for future developments.

2.4 Diffusion in a potential 

In the following sections, we investigate some applications of the previous results to particular choices of the diffusion process ̂ 𝐗 𝑡 concerning the Wiener process, the OU process and a linear process. For such cases, it is also useful to adopt a description in which the transformed diffusion process is viewed 13 as moving in a potential 𝑈 (𝐱 ) in 𝐶 1(). Hence, when the 2nd-order infinitesimal moments of 𝐗 𝑡 are constant, that is, when Σ( 𝐱 ) ≡ Σ, the SDE (2.1) for 𝐗 𝑡 can be expressed as

Moreover, the potential U has also been employed in one-dimensional settings, when featuring two (or more) minima, to model bimodal (or multimodal) diffusions (cf. Forman and Sørensen [14], Hongler et al. [23], and references therein), as well as in monotone cases, to represent dynamics influenced by attractive natural endpoints (see Di Crescenzo et al. [9]). In the multidimensional framework, in Li et al. # 6 Concluding remarks and perspectives 

This paper has been devoted to exploiting some treatable transformations between multidimensional dif-fusion processes, allowing the transition p.d.f. of the new process to be expressed in terms of a product 33 form involving the transition density of the former process. The manageability of the transformations allows to identify closed-form relations between the original and the transformed diffusion concerning several features of interest in the stochastic framework, such as stochastic ordering, Poissonian resetting or diffusion in a potential. The comparison based on the usual stochastic order, developed in Section 2.2, is particularly useful to identify situations in which the new diffusion process has significantly different behaviors compared to the original one, which is relevant in several applied contexts. It is worthwhile noting that the product-form relation between the transition p.d.f.’s is inherited also by the same processes subject to Poisson-paced resets. This fact discloses the possibility of applying the related expressions to further stochastic models of interest. Finally, the availability of a closed-form expression for the trans-formed process’ potential function is a useful tool to investigate features of the density functions. Throughout the paper, we focused on two case studies, referring to the transformation based on the Wiener process and the OU process. Other cases of interest are left to forthcoming investigations based, for instance, on the lognormal diffusion process (as extension of the case treated in Section 5.1 of Ricciardi et al. [41]). In both cases, the expression of the weight function 𝑤 is specified, hence the product-form relation between the p.d.f.’s is well-posed. The condition for the stochastic ordering of the original and transformed processes is well identified for both Wiener and OU cases, and the expression for the potential function of the transformed process is also given. In addition, the mathematical tractability of the density function in the Wiener case allows us to give further explicit results on the mixture form for the p.d.f. and the extention with Poissonian resets. In this framework, we pinpoint that Proposition 3.7 led to the stationary p.d.f. of the transformed Wiener process subject to reset. A gap in the literature prevented us by performing the same task for the transformed OU process, so this is an open question for future developments. Special attention has been devoted to the two-dimensional case. Firstly, we provided some results con-cerning the transition density for processes in ℝ2 with specific symmetry properties in the presence of an absorbing boundary. Subsequently, a further study is brought on for a particular class of processes, i.e. diffusions with linear drifts, which verify the previous symmetry condition. Focusing on such processes allows to obtain an expression for 𝑤 which is exploitable for a wider class of processes in addition to Wiener and OU. In the following we address other issues of interest for forthcoming studies. The possibility of using Eq.
